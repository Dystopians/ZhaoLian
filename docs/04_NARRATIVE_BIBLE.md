# 04 — Narrative Bible

## Premise

In a quiet West Sumatran town after Japan's announced surrender, the Chinese merchant Zhao Lian steps out in sleepwear and wooden clogs after a young man calls at his door. He never returns. The player reconstructs the preceding years and the later investigation while learning that Zhao Lian was the writer Yu Dafu—and that the distinction between the two names once kept him alive.

## Central dramatic question

Not “Who killed Yu Dafu?” but:

> When may a later editor turn disappearance into death, testimony into fact, and a useful alias into a commemorated name?

## Themes

- Name as shelter, exposure, and posthumous ownership.
- Translation as compromise, protection, betrayal, and evidence.
- The difference between victory on the radio and power in the street.
- The daily persistence of occupation institutions after political surrender.
- The ethics of completing an incomplete historical sentence.
- The gap between public martyr, local merchant, husband, friend, and witness.

## Protagonist layers

### Zhao Lian / “Zhao Boss”

Local social identity. Merchant, wine-maker, generous host, Japanese-speaking interpreter, husband, and a person whose relation to occupiers can look ambiguous.

### Yu Dafu / “Mr. Yu”

Known to fellow exiles and discovered by Japanese military police. Writer, anti-Japanese cultural worker, dangerous public identity, and later national memorial figure.

### The player/editor

Present-day, unnamed, not omniscient. They arrange sources and stage reconstructions. The interface must remind them when they move beyond materials.

## Supporting roles

Use role labels in the main text unless historically verified naming is necessary.

- **Wife:** a person waiting, managing a household, labor, uncertainty, and later memory. Naming variants must be explained in the appendix.
- **Friend in the house:** composite witness used to support conversation and search actions; explicitly disclosed.
- **Wine-factory worker:** composite local observer.
- **Released resident:** composite example of protective translation; never represented as a verified single rescue case.
- **Japanese military policeman:** composite for routine scenes; the identity-exposure anecdote is testimony-derived and must carry that class.
- **Young caller:** unresolved person; no motive is confirmed.
- **Coffee-shop worker:** testimony-based role.
- **Road witness/farmer:** testimony-based role with uncertainty about what could be seen.
- **Later investigator/editor:** interface layer drawing on Hu Yuzhi and subsequent research, not a fully impersonated historical first-person voice.

## Voice

- Third-person external or second-person archival address.
- Concrete nouns, short clauses, restrained metaphor.
- No imitation of Yu Dafu's confessional prose.
- No access to his final private thoughts.
- Avoid omniscient statements such as “he knew he would die.”
- Use repetition sparingly: names, translation, waiting, empty fields.

## Locked motifs

- Wine glass and ledger.
- A list of names.
- Dream speech.
- “赵先生” / “郁先生”.
- Radio static.
- Sleepwear and wooden clogs.
- A door crossed once.
- Food losing heat.
- A lamp wick becoming shorter.
- A newborn's date beside a father's unresolved status.
- Two death dates that cannot be merged.

## Locked lines

These lines may be polished only with explicit editorial approval:

1. `你不能改变这件事。你将改变它留下来的文字。`
2. `在小镇上，一个人与日本人说话。有人因此怀疑他。有人因此活下来。`
3. `白天，他替别人翻译。夜里，他担心自己的话被别人翻译。`
4. `郁先生。`
5. `那天以后，日本人知道赵廉是谁。赵廉也知道，日本人知道。第二天，酒厂仍然开门。`
6. `战争在广播里结束了。街上的汽车、枪和档案，还在日本人手中。`
7. `有点事情，我出去一趟。`
8. `女儿出生：1945年8月30日。父亲状态：尚未归来。`
9. `无论你如何编目，他都没有从门外回来。`

## Structural rule

The camera can follow Zhao Lian up to the threshold. Once he leaves, the narrative loses the right to enter his point of view or follow the vehicle. Every subsequent statement must come from waiting, testimony, document, or later reconstruction.
